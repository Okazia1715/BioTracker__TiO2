# Non-Technical Summary of Findings

The specific objectives of my analysis were to determine whether a
machine learning-based pipeline can reproducibly quantify particle
localization relative to segmented cells within gastrointestinal
tissue images.

Using a custom-trained Cellpose model, I was able to successfully
segment the adipocyte structures within the benign colon
histology images. Following iterative refinement, the segmented
images produced visually consistent results, which was critical to
ensure that the particle classification process was using reliable
cell boundaries.

To analyze the localization quantification process, I designed
two synthetic conditions, including a random particle placement
and an accumulation placement with a five-fold bias inside the
cell. These conditions are critical in demonstrating the ability
of the pipeline to detect differences between the two conditions.

The results show that the pipeline can successfully detect
differences between the two conditions using a variety of
quantitative metrics, including the number of particles inside
the cell, the number of particles on the cell boundary, the mean
number of particles per cell, distance to boundary, and the
number of clusters.

The summary plot generated by the application also shows
systematic shifts between the two groups, demonstrating the
reliability of the computational workflow in detecting the
introduced spatial bias.

## What I Am Confident About

I am confident that the segmentation pipeline is reproducible
across the entire dataset with fixed parameters. The logic behind
classifying particles, such as differentiating between
background, boundary, and inside-cell localization, is
consistent. The effect of the accumulation bias is also evident
in the quantitative results, and the exported figures correlate
with the visual overlays created for quality control purposes.

Reproducibility was tested by re-executing the analysis with the
same parameters, and the results remained the same.

## What I Am Less Certain About

However, there are significant sources of uncertainty that
restrict the degree to which the results can be interpreted
biologically. The quality of segmentation was assessed mainly
through visual inspection and loss convergence, as opposed to
more quantitative approaches such as Dice score or Intersection
over Union. This restricts the objective evaluation of
segmentation quality.

Furthermore, the artificial particles, which are the basis of
evaluation, do not correspond to actual nanoparticles in terms
of physical and biochemical properties. Instead, they are more
of a simulated environment. The lack of a ground truth
nanoparticle dataset also restricts the evaluation, as the
accuracy of localization cannot be directly referenced against
actual labels.

Additionally, the use of augmented images within the dataset may
also result in an evaluation that is not representative of actual
natural variations. Therefore, although I am confident in the
computational consistency of the pipeline, I would be cautious
when interpreting any biological results obtained at this stage.
