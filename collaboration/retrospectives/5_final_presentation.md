# Milestone 5 Retrospective

## Stop Doing

At this milestone, I found myself falling into the trap of
considering it as a technical report instead of a small and
concise presentation for my audience.

My initial outline was too packed with methodological details
instead of clarity, audience relevance, and impact.

Another thing I found myself doing was taking for granted that
the project’s impact would be obvious and did not spell out:

- The audience that would benefit from it
- The specific problem it solves
- The importance of it for reproducibility

Another thing I did wrong was not integrating the different
models of communication that we learned during the workshop
into my slide design as soon as possible. I focused on content
before considering its framing.

Things to Stop Doing:

- Adding too much technical detail into small presentations
- Assuming that the project’s impact will be obvious
- Separating ideas of communication from slide design

## Continue Doing

One strength I identified with my presentation was that I
connected the presentation to the real pain points, such as
the segmentation process and the absence of reproducible
quantification in microscopy analysis. This kept the
presentation real and believable.

The other strength I identified with the presentation was the
visual clarity, which was excellent. The workflow diagrams and
the outputs, such as the masks, overlays, and the CSV tables,
were clear.

I managed to emphasize the importance of reproducibility and
integrity, which is important to my audience.

So, I am going to continue doing:

- Connect the presentation to the real pain points, such as
  the segmentation process and the absence of reproducible
  quantification in microscopy analysis.
- Use visual clarity to untangle complex processes.
- Emphasize the importance of reproducibility and integrity.
- Keep the audience’s technical constraints in mind.

Another thing that I should do in the future is to make sure
that the validation results are more prominently included in
the narrative, and not just in the workflow.

I should also standardize how the presentation is linked and
summarized in the repository README to make sure that the
communication artifact is included in the project.

## Lessons Learned

This milestone made it clear that talking about science isn’t
a sidebar – it’s part of the system itself. A computational
pipeline only makes sense insofar as its purpose, its
boundaries, and its consequences are explained.

I learned that "simplifying" doesn’t mean "dumbing down." It
means emphasizing what really matters for the reader or user.

Communication and software design have a reciprocal
relationship. If a system can be explained simply, it’s
probably a well-designed system. And if a system needs to be
explained simply, it’s probably a well-designed system.

Most importantly, I learned that scientific software is built
on three legs that need to be aligned – technical soundness,
reproducible validation, and clear communication.

## Strategy vs. Board

### What parts of my plan went as expected?

The short talk worked well in tying together problem, solution,
audience, and impact in a way that felt natural.

The use of visuals and the emphasis on clarity and
reproducibility aligned well with the overall communication
strategy.

### What parts of my plan did not work out?

My original outline was too technical, more like a report than
a concise presentation.

I had not incorporated the communication models into my slide
design early enough.

### Did I need to add things that weren't in my strategy?

Yes. I needed to emphasize audience benefit more clearly,
tighten the problem statement, and sharpen the value
proposition.

I also needed to make validation results more prominent within
the narrative.

### Did I remove extra steps?

I removed significant methodological detail in order to make
the message simple, focused, and impactful within a short
presentation format.
