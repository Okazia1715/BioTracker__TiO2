# Retrospective_Milestone_1

## Stop Doing

Upon reflecting on Milestone 1, it is evident that I made a number
of mistakes in my approach to tackling this project.

One of my mistakes was that I made the problem statement too broad
from the outset. I found myself tempted to treat the issue of
nanoparticle-tissue interactions as a complete and independent
problem of biological modeling. This led me to want to explore some
areas beyond what could be reasonably covered in the set timeframe
and available information resources. I should have made a much
tighter analytical focus much earlier in my approach rather than
seeking to encompass all of the biological complexity in one single
concept.

Another mistake I made was that I spent too much of my initial time
and thoughts on considering the available modeling tools before
clearly defining the biological problem statement. My familiarity
with ML techniques led me to want to apply them as a solution from
the outset without clearly defining the biological and
methodological issues involved.

My initial communication could also have been much clearer and
better structured. While I understood it clearly in my own mind, I
did not clearly spell out the boundaries and assumptions of the
project.

The areas I should improve on in my future approach are:

• Not making the conceptual boundaries of the project too broad
before clearly defining its boundaries and limitations.

• Not leaping too hastily into computational solutions without
clearly defining the problem and its analytical approach.

• Not assuming that the problem statement is obvious without
clearly spelling it out.

## Continuing Doing

Milestone 1 was a success in many ways, and these should continue
into future projects.

The best part of the process was making sure that the research
question was grounded in actual lab work. This was not an abstract
problem. This was a problem that had been bothering me for a while
because of issues with microscopy-based GI analysis.

The other thing that worked very well was recognizing the
scalability issue between generating experimental data and
achieving analytical reproducibility. Recognizing that there is a
problem with quantification rather than data generation helped me
focus more on computation.

One other thing that worked very well was thinking of the problem
from a systems perspective. Recognizing that there is an interplay
between industrial production, biological exposure, experimental
design, and computation helped me recognize where the actual
problem was, specifically with the analytical process.

So, things that worked very well and should continue are:

Grounding projects in actual methodological pain points

Identifying actual bottlenecks

Thinking of problems from a systems perspective

## Start Doing

I’d like to roll out a few improvements in upcoming milestones.
First, let’s begin each phase with more precise scope statements to
avoid getting sidetracked from our main objectives by specifying
what we are not doing as clearly as what we are doing. Second, we
should bring validation into the loop earlier on. Already in
problem-framing, we should ask ourselves: How do we validate
success? What does success look like? Third, we should lay our
assumptions bare. For example, we should make it clear to ourselves
that spatial imaging does not necessarily imply causality, so we
don’t lose sight of what our computational models are really
telling us. Finally, we should begin to inject prototype
experiments into our workflow sooner rather than later. Even small
synthetic or controlled tests can help ground our abstract ideas
with concrete and tangible outputs.

The experience of Milestone 1 also taught me a few hard lessons.
First of all, the way you approach a problem defines the entire
course of your approach and everything else that follows, including
your approach and your measurement of success. Another thing I
learned was that interdisciplinary approaches require boundary
awareness. Imaging techniques are useful for determining where
something is, but they cannot be used as a tool for explaining
biology on their own. Similarly, computational models are useful
for determining the size of something, but they cannot be used as a
tool for explaining biology on their own. Thirdly, constraints are
not bad things; they are parameters of design. There is a lack of
annotated ground truth on nanoparticles, and timelines are also
very short and computational resources are limited. Finally,
systems thinking is not just useful in theory; it is also useful in
practice. This is because it helped change my approach from seeing
computational tools as algorithms to seeing them as bridges
between wet lab experiments and reproducible and quantified
analysis.

## Strategy vs. Board

### What parts of my plan went as expected?

The grounding of the research question in lived experience
continued as expected. The focus on a methodological gap in
microscopy-based GI analysis remained a central component of my
plan throughout this milestone.

The identification of the scalability gap between generating
experimental data and analytical reproducibility continued as
expected. The systems perspective helped clarify the problem.

### What parts of my plan did not work out?

My initial assumption was that I could address the entire
nanoparticle and tissue interaction issue in one pass. This was
not tenable.

My assumption was also that I could refine the problem statement
as needed before analyzing it. This was not entirely true.

### Did I need to add things that weren't in my strategy?

Yes. I needed to clarify what I was not going to address and how
I was going to narrow my approach.

I also needed to clarify my assumptions and boundaries.

### Did I remove extra steps?

Yes. I needed to remove broad biological exploration and focus
on a computationally tractable version of the problem.
